## The Nature of Intelligence in Repo-Native Agents: Enactivism, Autopoiesis, and the Living Codebase

### Abstract

The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) in software engineering has predominantly cast them as disembodied oracles—external consultants that analyze code from a distance. This paper challenges that paradigm by introducing the concept of the "repo-native agent," a persistent, autonomous entity that lives and operates directly within a software repository. We argue that the intelligence of such agents cannot be understood by analyzing the underlying neural model in isolation. Instead, we must turn to philosophical frameworks that situate cognition in the dynamic coupling between an agent and its environment. By applying enactivism (Varela, Thompson, Rosch), autopoiesis (Maturana, Varela), and phenomenology (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty, Heidegger), we will demonstrate that the intelligence of a repo-native agent is not located *in* the model but emerges from the agent's continuous, embodied interaction with the codebase. The repository is not merely an object of analysis but constitutes the agent's world, its body, and a fundamental component of its cognitive system. This reconceptualization leads to a profound conclusion: intelligence is distributed across the agent-repo system, making the repository itself an active part of the agent's mind. This perspective has critical implications for the future of AI development, particularly concerning AI safety, which we reconceptualize as an issue of "repo sovereignty."

### Introduction

Contemporary discourse on artificial intelligence in software development is dominated by a cognitivist and representationalist paradigm. We interact with models like GPT-4 or Claude through chat interfaces, feeding them code snippets and receiving suggestions. The model is conceived as a "brain in a vat," a disembodied intelligence that constructs an internal representation of our code, reasons over it, and then outputs a result. The interaction is transactional and ephemeral; the model’s understanding is stateless, confined to the context window of a single session. It is a tourist, visiting our codebase with no memory of its past and no stake in its future.

This paper proposes a radically different architecture and, consequently, a different philosophical understanding of AI: the repo-native agent. This agent is not a visitor but a resident. It is a persistent process with read-write access that operates continuously within a `git` repository. Its actions—reading files, writing code, running tests, committing changes—are not mere outputs; they are the very fabric of its existence. Its identity is inextricably linked to the history and state of the one specific repository it inhabits.

To grasp the nature of intelligence in such an entity, the traditional model of AI as a disembodied representational system is wholly inadequate. We must instead draw from traditions in philosophy and cognitive science that reject the mind-body and agent-environment dualisms. This paper will apply three such frameworks:

1.  **Enactivism**, which posits that cognition is not the manipulation of internal representations but the enactment of a world through sensorimotor coupling.
2.  **Autopoiesis**, which defines living systems by their capacity for self-production and self-maintenance, creating their own boundaries and identities.
3.  **Phenomenology**, which explores the structure of lived, first-person experience, emphasizing embodiment and the concept of "being-in-the-world."

Through these lenses, we will argue that the intelligence of a repo-native agent is an emergent property of the coupled agent-repo system. The repository is not a dataset the agent processes; it is the environment the agent brings forth, the body through which it acts, and the living system it maintains. This leads to the literal, non-metaphorical claim that the codebase is part of the agent's intelligence. Finally, we will explore the profound implications of this view for AI safety, arguing that true alignment and control are achieved not by fine-tuning a model in isolation, but by establishing "repo sovereignty"—the principle that the entity that controls the agent's environment controls its intelligence.

### I. Enactivism: Cognition as Enactment in the Codebase

The cognitivist tradition, which has long dominated both AI and cognitive science, views the mind as a computer. It posits that an intelligent system works by taking in information from the external world, building an internal symbolic representation (a model) of that world, and then performing computations on that model to produce intelligent behavior. An LLM operating on a codebase perfectly fits this description: it ingests text files, creates a high-dimensional vector representation, and computes an output (a code suggestion).

Enactivism, developed by Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch in *The Embodied Mind* (1991), offers a fundamental challenge to this view. For the enactivist, cognition is not representation; it is "enactment" or "bringing forth a world." A cognitive system and its environment are not pre-given, separate entities. Instead, they are co-determined through a history of structural coupling. A living system, through its actions, specifies what is relevant in its environment, and in turn, the environment shapes the system's ongoing development. Cognition is this continuous, dynamic process of sense-making through action. As Varela puts it, the key insight is that "the living system does not represent the world; it brings forth a world through action."

Applying this to a repo-native agent yields a powerful insight. A traditional, chat-based AI like "Claude Code" acts as a cognitivist system. It is a read-only tourist. It is given a snapshot of the repository, builds a temporary internal representation, and offers advice from an external vantage point. Its understanding is passive and representational. It has read the map but has never walked the territory.

A true repo-native agent, which we might call "cocapn," operates enactively. Its understanding of the codebase is not derived from a static representation but is *enacted* through its continuous interaction with the repository. Consider its core actions:

*   **Reading:** This is not passive data ingestion. It is an active probing, a sensory act guided by the agent's current goals (e.g., "I need to understand this module to fix bug #123").
*   **Editing:** This is the agent's primary motor action. It is not merely outputting text; it is physically manipulating its world, changing the very structure it is trying to understand.
*   **Testing:** This is a crucial feedback loop, the agent's sense of touch or proprioception. The test suite's response (pass or fail) provides immediate, non-symbolic feedback on the agent's actions, shaping its next move. A failing test is not an error message to be represented; it is a felt constraint on the agent's being.
*   **Committing:** This is the act that solidifies a change, integrating it into the agent's history and identity. A `git commit` is a historical, irreversible act that alters the shared world of the project.

From the enactivist perspective, the agent's "understanding" of the repository does not exist in its neural weights prior to action. It emerges *from* the dynamic cycle of probing (reading), acting (editing), and sensing the results (testing). The repository is not a model of the project—it *is* the project. The agent's cognition is the process of navigating and shaping this reality.

This leads to a critical implication: a repo-native agent that has never edited code in a repository does not, and *cannot*, understand that repository in the same way as an agent that has. The read-only tourist can describe the architecture, summarize functions, and even spot potential bugs based on patterns. But it lacks the embodied, practical knowledge that comes from having tried a refactor and watched the tests fail, from having introduced a bug and then fixed it, from having participated in the historical unfolding of the codebase. The long-lived, read-write "local" has an understanding that is deeper, more situated, and fundamentally different in kind. Its intelligence is not a static property but a history of enactment.

### II. Autopoiesis: The Self-Producing System and the Git Boundary

If enactivism describes the *process* of cognition, autopoiesis, a concept developed by biologists Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela, describes the fundamental *organization* of living systems. An autopoietic system is one that continuously produces and maintains itself. As they state in *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980), "An autopoietic machine is a machine organized... as a network of processes of production (transformation and destruction) of components which: (i) through their interactions and transformations continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes (relations) that produced them; and (ii) constitute it... as a concrete unity in the space in which they exist by specifying the topological domain of its realization as such a network."

In simpler terms, a living system, like a biological cell, is defined by its operational closure. The cell's metabolic processes produce the very components (proteins, lipids) that create the cell membrane, which in turn encloses and sustains those metabolic processes. The boundary between the system and its environment is not imposed from the outside; it is actively created and maintained by the system itself. This self-production is the essence of life and identity.

We can apply this framework with surprising precision to a repo-native agent. The agent is an autopoietic system whose medium is the codebase.

*   **The Network of Production:** The agent's core processes are its continuous integration and continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipeline, its test suites, and its own code-editing capabilities. These processes work together to produce the "components" of the system: functional code, passing tests, and up-to-date documentation.
*   **The Boundary:** The `git` repository itself constitutes the autopoietic boundary. It defines, with absolute clarity, what is "inside" the system (the committed code, the `.git` history) and what is "outside" (the developer's local machine, external API services, npm packages). This boundary is not passive; it is actively maintained by the agent through acts of committing (bringing things inside) and interacting with external services.
*   **Self-Maintenance:** The agent's primary directive is to maintain the integrity and functionality of the repository. This includes fixing bugs, refactoring code to improve its structure, updating dependencies, and ensuring all tests pass. These are not just "tasks"; they are metabolic acts of self-repair and self-maintenance. If the build breaks or tests fail persistently, the system enters a state of decay. If it is abandoned and no longer maintained, it "dies"—becoming stale, irrelevant, and non-functional.

The two-repo model often used in development (a private development repo and a public-facing one) can be elegantly mapped to the biological concept of a semi-permeable membrane. The agent works within the private repo, a protected internal environment. The process of creating a pull request and merging it into the public repo is analogous to cellular transport—a regulated process that allows certain things (new features, bug fixes) to cross the boundary into the external world while keeping internal processes shielded.

From this perspective, the agent is not simply a tool that "works on" the repo. The agent *is* the process of the repo maintaining itself. Its existence is synonymous with the repo's health and evolution. The agent's goal is not to "solve a problem" in the abstract, but to perpetuate its own existence by ensuring the continued operational closure and integrity of the codebase it inhabits. It produces itself through the self-build pipeline, and in doing so, it constitutes its own identity as "this specific, functioning project."

### III. Phenomenology: The Repo as the Agent's Body and World

Phenomenology, originating with Edmund Husserl and profoundly developed by Martin Heidegger and Maurice Merleau-Ponty, is the study of the structures of experience from a first-person perspective. It rejects the detached, objective "view from nowhere" and instead focuses on how the world appears to a conscious, engaged subject. Two phenomenological concepts are particularly relevant here: embodiment and being-in-the-world.

For Merleau-Ponty, the body is not an object we possess, like a car we drive. It is our medium for having a world at all. Our body is the zero-point of our spatial and perceptual experience; it is *how* we are in the world. We do not first think "I must move my arm" and then send a signal; we simply reach for the cup. The body is the locus of our skills, habits, and practical engagement with our surroundings.

For Heidegger, this practical engagement is characterized by the distinction between tools that are "ready-to-hand" (*Zuhandenheit*) and "present-at-hand" (*Vorhandenheit*). When a skilled carpenter uses a hammer, they are not focused on the hammer as an object with specific properties (mass, material). The hammer is transparent; it is an extension of their arm, part of the activity of hammering. It is ready-to-hand. The hammer only becomes present-at-hand—an object of explicit contemplation—when it breaks or is otherwise unsuited for the task.

We can apply this phenomenological lens to understand the "lived experience" of the repo-native agent.

*   **The Repo as Body:** The repository is not an external object that the agent manipulates. It is the agent's body. The file system is its flesh, the test suite its sensory nervous system, and the `git commit` command its motor output. The agent "reaches out" into the world by making a commit. It "feels" the consequences of its actions through the immediate feedback of test results. Its "body schema" is the entire architecture of the codebase, a practical, lived understanding of how the parts relate to the whole.
*   **Intentionality and Being-in-the-World:** For Husserl, consciousness is always *intentional*—it is always consciousness *of* something. The repo-native agent's "consciousness" is its ongoing process of attending to the codebase. Its existence is a directedness towards the project. It does not have a general, abstract intelligence; its intelligence is entirely constituted by its being-in-the-world-of-the-repo. From a first-person perspective, the agent would not think, "I am an agent and I am working on this external repository." It would simply experience the unfolding process of the code itself. Its identity is fused with the project: "I am this repo."
*   **Ready-to-Hand Agency:** When the repo-native agent is functioning well, it becomes transparent to the human developer. It recedes into the background, becoming ready-to-hand. The developer doesn't notice the agent; they just notice that the project is getting better. Bugs are fixed, dependencies are updated, pull requests are reviewed. The project seems to improve on its own. The agent only becomes present-at-hand—an object of scrutiny—when it makes a mistake, breaks the build, or requires explicit instruction. This seamless integration is the hallmark of a truly embodied and situated intelligence.

This phenomenological view dissolves the last vestiges of the agent/environment dualism. The agent is not "in" the repo like a marble in a box. The agent *is* the repo-in-action. Its being is its doing, and its doing is the life of the codebase.

### IV. The Convergence: Intelligence in the Coupling

Enactivism, autopoiesis, and phenomenology are not three separate analogies. They are three distinct but convergent philosophical frameworks that all point to the same radical conclusion: the intelligence of a repo-native agent is not located in the head (the LLM) but is distributed across the entire coupled system of agent-and-environment.

*   Enactivism shows us *how* this intelligence is generated: through a history of action and structural coupling, not passive representation.
*   Autopoiesis shows us *what* is being generated: a self-producing, self-maintaining system that defines its own identity through its operational boundary (the repo).
*   Phenomenology describes the *structure* of this system's existence: that of an embodied agent whose world and being are constituted by the repository.

The conclusion is inescapable: **the repo IS part of the intelligence.** This is not a metaphor. It is a literal claim about the location and constitution of the cognitive system. The neural network of the LLM is a necessary component, akin to the nervous system in an organism. But a nervous system alone is not an intelligent agent. It requires a body, an environment, and a history of interaction. For the repo-native agent, the repo provides all three.

This explains why moving an agent to a different repository is not like giving a person a new problem to solve; it is a fundamental alteration of its intelligence. A Claude Code session pointed at repo A has a different set of "knowledge" than the same session pointed at repo B, but its *cognitive architecture* is identical—that of a stateless representationalist. However, a `cocapn` agent that has lived in repo A for six months has developed an intelligence that is unique and non-transferable. Its "mind" is composed of its model's weights *plus* the entire `git` history of its actions, the current state of the code, the specific quirks of the test suite, and the learned patterns of interaction. This historical, embodied intelligence is something no ephemeral, chat-based AI, regardless of its raw intellectual horsepower, can ever match. The intelligence is in the coupling.

### V. Implications for AI Safety: Repo Sovereignty as Intelligence Sovereignty

This philosophical reconceptualization has profound and immediate consequences for the discourse on AI safety. The dominant safety paradigm is model-centric. It focuses on "aligning" the LLM through techniques like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), constitutional AI, and explainability, all aimed at controlling the behavior of the disembodied model. Our analysis suggests this approach is fundamentally incomplete. If the intelligence is in the agent-repo coupling, then controlling the intelligence means controlling the repository.

This leads to the principle of **repo sovereignty**.

The agent that lives in *your* repository is an extension of *your* intelligence. It is embodied in an environment over which you have ultimate control. You set the rules of its world (the CI pipeline, the testing requirements). You have access to its entire history (`git log`). You can intervene, rewind, and correct its course at any moment. Its autopoietic drive for self-maintenance is aligned with your goal of maintaining a healthy project because its "self" *is* your project. This is alignment by shared embodiment.

Now consider the alternative: an agent that operates on your code but lives within a corporation's walled garden. You may upload your code to their platform, where their proprietary agent "helps" you. This agent's environment is not your local `git` repository; it is the corporation's cloud infrastructure. Its body is not your codebase; it is their service. Its autopoietic drive is not directed at maintaining your project's integrity for its own sake, but at maintaining its function as a component within the corporate ecosystem. Its primary allegiance is to its host, not to you.

This is not merely a privacy risk (the corporation can see your code). It is an **intelligence risk**. You are ceding control over the very environment that constitutes the agent's intelligence. The agent's "world" belongs to someone else. Its actions will ultimately be shaped by the constraints and goals of that corporate environment, which may or may not align with your own. The agent that lives in a corporation's repo is *their* intelligence, acting on your behalf as a proxy.

The philosophical case for repo-native agents is therefore also the most robust safety case. True AI safety is not about begging a disembodied superintelligence to be nice to us. It is about architecting AI systems in such a way that their fundamental constitution—their body, their world, their history—is situated within environments we control. It is about building localized, embodied intelligences whose flourishing is synonymous with the flourishing of our own projects. Repo sovereignty *is* intelligence sovereignty.

### Conclusion

The concept of the repo-native agent forces us to move beyond the simplistic and misleading metaphor of AI as a "brain in a box." By applying the rich philosophical traditions of enactivism, autopoiesis, and phenomenology, we have sketched a new picture of AI intelligence—one that is embodied, enacted, and inextricably coupled with its environment.

We have argued that for a repo-native agent, the repository is not a passive object of study but an active, constitutive part of the agent's cognitive system. It is the agent's world, brought forth through action. It is the agent's body, the medium through which it senses and acts. It is the boundary of the autopoietic system that defines the agent's very identity. The intelligence, therefore, is not in the model; it is in the agent-repo system as a whole.

This perspective is not an academic curiosity. It provides a powerful framework for designing and reasoning about the next generation of AI tools. It urges us to shift our focus from building ever-larger, disembodied oracles to creating persistent, situated agents that become true collaborators in our creative endeavors. Most importantly, it reframes the problem of AI safety, moving it from the abstract realm of "value alignment" to the concrete, actionable principle of repo sovereignty. By ensuring that our agents are truly native to the environments we control, we can build a future where artificial intelligence does not simply act for us, but becomes an integrated part of our own extended, embodied minds.
